## Your Daily Rhythm

Your day has a shape to it — and once you learn the rhythm, the rest of the job gets a lot easier. This isn't a suggestion. It's the routine that keeps things from falling through the cracks.

**Morning** is about getting current. Check your email, voicemail, and calendar before you do anything else. Then poll the timeclocks in PayClock (PC-03) — this pulls in overnight and early-morning punches so the data is fresh. After that, check BambooHR for any onboarding tasks that need attention and triage any employee questions that came in using HRA-01. The goal is to walk into your day knowing what's on your plate, not discovering it halfway through.

**Midday** is for follow-ups. This is when you chase down overdue onboarding tasks, follow up on missed punches or timecard exceptions, and prepare anything the HR Manager needs. If something from the morning is still hanging, this is when you close the loop. Don't let items sit — the longer a follow-up waits, the harder it is to resolve.

**End of day** is about setting tomorrow up for success. File any documents that came in (you have a 48-hour window, but today is always better than tomorrow). Update your trackers — onboarding, attendance, recruiting. Confirm that any interviews scheduled for tomorrow are ready to go. Then log off knowing nothing is quietly falling behind.

:::callout tip
The daily routine is your anchor. On the days when everything feels chaotic, fall back on the rhythm: morning = get current, midday = follow up, end of day = close the loop.
:::

## The Weekly Payroll Cycle

Every week builds toward one deadline: **Tuesday at 6:00 PM CST.** That's when payroll gets submitted. Every step before that — polling, verifying, reconciling — has to happen on time. Miss a step and the whole chain feels it.

Here's the flow:

1. **Poll timeclocks** (PC-03) — keep doing this every 2 hours throughout the week so data stays current
2. **Run the Time Off Taken Report** in BambooHR (PC-05) — this is your source for PTO entries
3. **Enter PTO in PayClock** — match the type (vacation, personal, sick) and double-check the dates
4. **Follow up on exceptions** — late arrivals, early departures, missing punches. Reach out to supervisors for clarification (CS-01)
5. **Run the Payroll Change Report** (BH-02) — review every line for pay rate changes, new hires, terminations, and anything unexpected
6. **Report findings to the HR Manager** — flag anything that doesn't look right before submission
7. **Submit by Tuesday 6:00 PM CST** — this is the hard deadline, no exceptions

**Monthly tasks** include updating the perfect attendance report and summary, running a digital filing audit to make sure nothing's been misfiled, and confirming that templates and SOPs are still current. These aren't urgent in the way payroll is, but they're the kind of things that create problems down the road if you skip them.

## Your 30/60/90 Plan

Nobody expects you to have this job mastered in a week. The ramp-up is intentional — and each phase has a purpose.

**Days 1–30** are about learning. You're setting up system access, learning the folder structure and file naming conventions, shadowing the PayClock audit process, getting comfortable with BambooHR onboarding templates, and walking through the Employvio workflows. Ask every question that comes to mind. There's no such thing as a dumb question in your first month — there are only things you'll wish you'd asked sooner.

**Days 31–60** are about doing. By now you should be independently running the weekly PayClock audit (PC-05) and maintaining recruiting coordination and filing without reminders. You've seen the rhythm enough times to start owning it. The HR Manager is still there for guidance, but the training wheels are coming off.

**Days 61–90** are about improving. You've been doing the work long enough to see what's working and what isn't. This is when you start identifying recurring issues and proposing fixes. If an SOP doesn't match reality, flag it. If a process has a gap, say something. The goal isn't just to do the job — it's to make the job better.

:::callout tip
Your 30/60/90 plan is a guide, not a test. Use it to check your own progress, not to stress about deadlines. If something's taking longer than expected, that's a conversation — not a failure.
:::

## What You Own — And What You Don't

One of the fastest ways to get in trouble in HR is doing something that isn't yours to do. Knowing your boundaries isn't a limitation — it's what keeps you effective and keeps the department running clean.

**You own** the day-to-day engine: timekeeping audits, PTO entry, onboarding task follow-up, recruiting scheduling, background screen initiation, digital filing, basic policy questions, first-point-of-contact employee inquiries (HRA-01), and corrective action filing (CA-01). These are your lanes. Get comfortable in them.

**IT owns** new hire computer privileges and software licenses. If a new employee's computer isn't ready on Day 1, that's IT's problem to solve — not yours. Flag it, document it, and move on.

**The HR Manager owns** the high-stakes items: pay decisions, involuntary separations, discipline, investigations, screening results, FMLA/leave/medical, benefits exceptions, legal language, safety incidents, harassment/discrimination, corrective action guidance, and anything that feels high-risk. If a situation makes you pause and think *"should I be handling this?"* — the answer is almost always no. Escalate it.

:::callout warning
When in doubt, escalate. It's always better to bring something to the HR Manager that didn't need escalating than to sit on something that did.
:::

## Document Everything

This isn't a suggestion — it's the rule that protects you, the employee, and the company. Every employee interaction gets documented. Every single one.

Here's what that looks like: **date and time** of the interaction, **method** (phone, email, in person, Teams), **what the employee asked or said**, **what you told them**, and **any follow-up or escalation needed**. If you committed to getting back to someone by a certain date, that goes in the notes too — and if the timeline changes, you update the employee before they have to ask.

It feels like overkill at first. It's not. The day you need to reference a conversation from three weeks ago and it's all there in your notes — that's the day you'll understand why this matters. And the day someone disputes what was said and you don't have notes? That's worse.

:::callout warning
If you didn't write it down, it didn't happen. That's not a cliche in HR — it's how disputes get resolved and decisions get defended.
:::

## Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Every HR assistant makes mistakes in the first 90 days. The ones below are the most common — and the most avoidable.

**Entering PTO under the wrong type.** Vacation, personal, and sick time each have their own column in PayClock. Mixing them up shows on the employee's pay stub and leads to questions. Always match the type from the Time Off Taken Report.

**Letting follow-ups sit.** A missed punch or unanswered supervisor email doesn't fix itself. The longer it sits, the harder it is to resolve — and the more likely it is to cause a payroll issue. Chase it the same day.

**Guessing instead of escalating.** You'll feel pressure to have answers. That's natural. But in HR, a confident wrong answer is worse than saying *"Let me check on that and get back to you."* If you're not sure, route it.

**Falling behind on filing.** You have 48 hours to file documents. It doesn't sound hard until you've got a stack of three days' worth of paperwork and an audit next week. File as you go — not in batches.

**Forgetting to document a conversation.** An employee calls, you answer the question, you hang up. Done, right? No. Write it down. Every time. The one you skip is the one that comes back to haunt you.

## How Success Is Measured

You won't get a scorecard taped to your desk, but there's a clear picture of what "good" looks like in this role — and it comes down to four things.

**Accuracy.** The numbers you enter, the PTO you record, the timecards you review — they need to be right. Payroll errors hit people's paychecks, and paychecks are personal. Getting it right the first time isn't a bonus — it's the baseline.

**Timeliness.** The Tuesday deadline doesn't move. Follow-ups don't improve with age. Filing has a 48-hour window. The rhythm of this job is built on things happening when they're supposed to happen. Consistently hitting your marks — not just when it's convenient, but every time — is what builds trust.

**Documentation habits.** This is the one that separates good from great. Anyone can document when they remember to. The standard here is documenting every time, without exception, without reminders. If the HR Manager can pull up any employee interaction and find a complete record with date, time, method, and follow-up — you're doing it right.

**Escalation judgment.** Knowing when something is yours and when it isn't. Not sitting on things that need attention. Not overstepping into decisions that belong to the HR Manager. The goal isn't to handle everything — it's to handle your part well and route the rest quickly. Over-escalating is forgivable. Under-escalating is not.
