## Quality, Accuracy & Your Paycheck

The RF scanner does most of the heavy lifting — but it cannot catch human error. This module covers production incentives, how accuracy connects directly to your pay check, and the common error types that slow you down. Getting it right is not just good work — it is good money. The fewer mistakes you make, the faster you move and the more you earn.

## Why Accuracy Matters

The production quota for Order Processors is 250 lines per hour. That means 250 different medications pulled, scanned, and staged — every hour. It sounds like a lot, but do not let that number freak you out. Most Order Processors are meeting production after just one or two weeks. And if it takes you a little longer, that is fine — you get 90 days to hit the mark.

Here is where it gets interesting. For every line you pull over the production quota, you earn a $1 bonus on your check. Pull an average of 350 lines per hour? That is a $100 bonus. Not too shabby.

But accuracy is the catch. Every error deducts 25% from that bonus. Remember that $100 example? Two errors in a pay period cuts it in half — down to $50. That is why accuracy matters. It is not just about doing good work. It is about protecting the money you already earned.

Focus on accuracy first. Accuracy is a habit. Speed comes with time.

## What the Scanner Catches (And What It Does Not)

The RF scanner keeps you on the right track. It verifies items, lots, locations, quantities, and baskets in real time — and it will stop you when something does not match. That is a powerful safety net.

But do not let it create a false sense of security. The scanner cannot catch everything.

**The scanner catches:**
- Wrong item scanned
- Wrong lot number
- Wrong location
- Wrong basket (each basket on your cart represents a separate order)
- Wrong quantity entered

**The scanner cannot catch:**
- **Over-pulls and under-pulls** — you enter the right quantity on the gun, but physically grab too many or too few
- **Mis-pulls** — you scan the correct product, but grab the wrong one off the shelf
- **Wrong basket placement** — the right product goes into the wrong basket on your cart

These are the gaps where your attention matters most. The scanner handles the data. You handle the details.

## Where Errors Actually Come From

Most errors do not happen because someone does not know the process. They happen because someone skips a step they have done a hundred times.

Here is what that usually looks like:

- **Rushing near quota** — you are close to hitting your number, so you speed up and stop double-checking. That is exactly when mistakes slip through.
- **Reading what you expect, not what is there** — you have pulled the same product all day, so your eyes see what they expect instead of what the label actually says.
- **Assuming the quantity** — you grab what looks right instead of counting. Close enough is not close enough when it is someone's medication.
- **Not verifying after the scan** — the scanner confirms the item matches, but it cannot confirm you grabbed the right quantity or put it in the right basket. The scan is not the last step — it is one step.
- **Distractions** — after training and your probationary period, you are allowed to use one earbud on the floor. Music can make the day move faster, but it can also pull your focus at the wrong moment. Stay aware of when your attention drifts — that is usually when errors happen.
- **Autopilot mode** — the work becomes routine, and routine becomes comfortable. Comfortable is where careless lives.

The fix is not complicated. It is discipline. Follow the process every time — even when you feel confident. Especially when you feel confident.

:::callout tip
The shortcuts that feel like efficiency often just move the problem downstream — where it takes twice as long to fix.
:::

## When Something Looks Wrong

You are going to run into situations where something does not add up. Maybe the product on the shelf does not match what the scanner is asking for. Maybe the quantity looks off. Maybe the packaging is damaged or the label is hard to read. Maybe the location is empty when it should not be.

When that happens — do not guess. Do not "make it work." This is medication, not office supplies. Getting creative with a problem you do not fully understand is how small issues become big ones.

**Here is what to do:**

1. **Stop** — do not try to force the pick or move on without resolving it
2. **Verify** — check the label, the location, and the scanner display again
3. **Ask** — if it still does not add up, bring it to your supervisor or trainer

Flagging problems is not a sign of being slow. It is a sign of paying attention. Nobody is going to be upset that you asked a question. The goal is catching problems early — and that starts with speaking up when something does not look right.

## Building Good Habits From Day One

You have learned what goes wrong and why. Now here is how to make sure accuracy becomes your default — not something you have to think about.

- **Read the label every time** — even when you think you know what it is. Especially when you think you know what it is.
- **Count every pick** — do not eyeball it. Close enough is not close enough when it is someone's medication.
- **Double-check before you drop** — before placing a product in a basket, confirm it is the right one. A quick glance takes a second. A mis-pull takes much longer to fix.
- **Slow down at the end of your shift** — fatigue is real, and the last hour is where mistakes like to hide.
- **Treat every pick like it is someone's medication** — because it is.

None of this is complicated. It is just consistency. The people who rarely make errors are not smarter. They just never skip the basics.

## It All Adds Up

You now know how the production bonus works — and what errors cost you. You know what the scanner catches and where the gaps are. You know where mistakes come from and how to build the habits that prevent them.

The difference between a good day and a great day is not speed. It is consistency. The people who earn the biggest bonuses are not the ones who rush. They are the ones who never skip the basics.

Read the label. Count the pick. Check the basket. Every time.

Accuracy is the easiest habit that pays you back.
