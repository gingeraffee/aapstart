## What This Module Covers

Accuracy is not extra credit. It is the job.

This module explains why quality matters, where errors usually happen, what to do when something looks wrong, and how small catches prevent bigger messes. Good warehouse work is not just fast. It is correct.

## Why Accuracy Matters

Wrong item. Wrong quantity. Wrong label. Wrong location.

Tiny mistake, giant ripple effect.

Accuracy protects the customer, the company, and the coworkers who rely on the step before theirs being done right. The good news is that most errors are preventable when the process is followed every time — not just on days when everything lines up perfectly.

## Quality Checkpoints

Quality should not depend on one lucky person noticing a problem at the last second.

That is why good operations build checkpoints into the workflow. Each step exists to confirm, catch, and prevent errors before they move downstream and multiply. When you are at a checkpoint, treat it like one — not like a formality to get through faster.

## Common Error Types and How to Avoid Them

Most warehouse errors are not dramatic. They are ordinary little shortcuts that snowball.

- Misread label
- Skipped scan
- Wrong location
- Assumed quantity
- Rushed packout

The fix is usually not genius. It is discipline. Follow the process every time, even when you feel confident. Especially when you feel confident.

:::callout tip
The shortcuts that feel like efficiency often just move the problem downstream — where it costs twice as much to fix.
:::

## When Something Is Wrong

Finding a problem is not the issue. Ignoring it is.

If something looks off — a label does not match, a quantity seems wrong, a location does not look right — stop, verify, and escalate the right way. A few extra minutes now beats a full rework situation later.

**When in doubt:**

1. Stop
2. Verify what you can
3. Ask your supervisor or trainer before proceeding

## Rework and Error Correction

Rework is what happens when an earlier mistake comes back wearing steel-toe consequences.

The goal is not just fixing the order in front of you. The goal is understanding what went wrong so the same issue does not keep making encore appearances. When rework happens, pay attention to the cause — not just the fix.

## Continuous Improvement

The people doing the work usually spot the problems first.

If a step is confusing, a process keeps causing errors, or something feels clunky for no good reason — say something. Good ideas do not only live in conference rooms. If you notice a pattern, bring it to your supervisor. That kind of attention is what makes operations better over time.
