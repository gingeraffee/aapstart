## The Stuff Everyone Assumes You Know

This module covers how performance is measured, what to wear, the open door policy, and conduct and overtime expectations. The practical stuff — so nothing catches you off guard.

## What To Do (and what not to do)

AAP wants to give you everything you need to succeed — and that starts with being clear about what success actually looks like here.

**What strong performance looks like**

Your supervisor will check in with you on an ongoing basis, with more formal evaluations at key points like the end of your introductory period. No surprises — that's the goal. Here's what they're looking for:

- **Accuracy** — Get it right the first time. Double-check when it matters.
- **Dependability** — Show up when you say you will and follow through on what you commit to.
- **Responsiveness** — Don't leave people waiting. A quick reply goes a long way.
- **Initiative** — See something that needs doing? Handle it without being asked.
- **Cooperation** — Work with people, not around them. Be easy to collaborate with.
- **Consistency** — Deliver the same quality on a Tuesday in March as you do during your first week.
- **Teamwork** — Support the people around you. Their wins are your wins.
- **Positive attitude** — Energy is contagious. Bring the kind you'd want to work next to.
- **Showing up** — Literally and figuratively. Be present, be engaged, be reliable.

**What corrective action looks like**

If performance or conduct issues come up, AAP uses a structured corrective action process. Things that can trigger it include insubordination, carelessness, unsatisfactory productivity, handbook or code of conduct violations, and poor attitude toward coworkers or work.

For attendance specifically, the point system has its own warning track:

| Points | Action |
|--------|--------|
| 5.0 points | Coaching |
| 6.0 points | Verbal warning |
| 7.0 points | Written warning |
| 8.0 points | Termination |

Supervisor discretion applies at each stage.

## Social Media and AAP

Your personal social media accounts are yours — but anything you post can reflect on AAP, especially if your profile identifies you as an employee. AAP doesn't monitor your accounts, but the company does expect you to use good judgment.

A few things to keep in mind:

- Don't share confidential company information, internal documents, or non-public business details
- Avoid posting anything that could be seen as harassment, discrimination, or a threat — even if it's meant as a joke
- If you mention AAP or your role, make it clear the views are your own
- Think before you post: if it would make your supervisor uncomfortable reading it aloud in a meeting, it probably doesn't belong online

This isn't about limiting your voice. It's about protecting your reputation and the company's. When in doubt, leave it out.

## Dress Code

:::track [warehouse]
AAP expects all employees to maintain a neat and clean appearance. Here are the most important bits of the dress code policy in our API Warehouses:

- Closed-toe, closed-heel shoes only
- No hats, head coverings, or bulky jackets
- No food or drinks on the warehouse floor
- Employee badges must be on your person and visible at all times

The shoe and clothing rules aren't arbitrary — they're about keeping the warehouse secure and everyone safe. If you need a snack or a drink, the break room is fully stocked and always available.
:::

:::track [administrative]
You don't need a suit, but you do need to look like you're here on purpose. Business casual Monday through Thursday, casual on Fridays. Jeans and a t-shirt are totally fine on Friday — just make sure they're clean, presentable, and hole-free. One thing that applies every day: **your badge stays on and visible.**
:::
