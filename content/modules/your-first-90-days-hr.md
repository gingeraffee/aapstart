## The Shape of Your First 90 Days

Nobody expects you to walk in and run this department. The first three months have a rhythm to them — learn, then do, then improve. Each phase builds on the one before it, and rushing through one to get to the next usually backfires.

Here's how it breaks down, what's expected at each stage, and how to know if you're on track.

## Days 1–30: Learn the Machine

Your first month is about absorbing, not performing. You're learning how things work, where things live, and who does what. The goal isn't to be fast — it's to be accurate when you start doing things on your own.

**What your days should look like:**

- **Complete AAP Start.** Every module, every quiz, every acknowledgement. This isn't busywork — it's the foundation for everything else you'll do. The policies, systems, and workflows in these modules are the ones you'll use every single day.
- **Learn the core systems.** BambooHR, PayClock, Employvio, Paylocity. You don't need to master them yet, but you should be able to log in, navigate, and understand what each one does. Bookmark them.
- **Shadow Nicole.** Watch how she handles things. Pay attention to the rhythm — the daily checks, the weekly payroll cycle, the way she responds to employee questions. You're not just learning tasks. You're learning judgment.
- **Meet the people.** Get to know the supervisors, the IT contacts, the warehouse managers. These are the people you'll email, call, and follow up with every week. A name attached to a face makes everything easier.
- **Read the Employee Handbook.** Not cover to cover in one sitting, but enough that you know what's in there and where to find it. Employees will ask you policy questions — you need to know where the answer lives, even if you don't have it memorized yet.

:::callout tip
Ask every question that comes to mind. There's no such thing as a dumb question in your first month — there are only things you'll wish you'd asked sooner.
:::

**How to know you're on track at day 30:** You can find what you need in the core systems. You know the daily routine. You can name the key people and what they handle. And you've built the habit of writing things down.

## Days 31–60: Start Doing the Work

Month two is where observation turns into action. You've seen the workflows enough times to start owning them. Nicole is still there for guidance, but the training wheels are coming off.

**What shifts in this phase:**

- **Own the routine tasks.** Timecard follow-ups, onboarding paperwork tracking, PTO entry, document filing — these are yours now. You should be completing them without step-by-step guidance on things you've done before.
- **Handle common employee questions.** You know where the policies are. You know the PTO rules, the attendance point values, the benefits timeline. Start answering the straightforward questions yourself — and route the ones that aren't yours.
- **Recognize the patterns.** By week five or six, you'll start noticing what repeats. The same timecard exceptions every pay period. The same benefits questions from new hires at the 60-day mark. The same follow-up emails you send every Tuesday. Seeing the patterns is how you get ahead of the work instead of reacting to it.
- **Flag your own gaps.** If there's something you still feel shaky on, say so. Bringing a gap to Nicole's attention in week six is smart. Hiding it until it causes a problem in week ten is not.

**"Increasing independence" doesn't mean doing things alone without support.** It means completing tasks you've already been trained on without needing someone to walk you through them again. You'll still have questions — the difference is they're more specific now. Less *"how do I do this?"* and more *"I did this, but I want to check one thing before I send it."*

:::callout tip
If your supervisor hasn't checked in on a task you've been handling for a few weeks, that's usually a good sign — it means they trust the work. Keep going.
:::

**How to know you're on track at day 60:** You can answer employee questions confidently. You handle routine tasks without reminders. When something comes up that isn't yours, you route it quickly instead of sitting on it.

## Days 61–90: Make It Better

By month three, you've been doing the job long enough to see what works and what doesn't. This is when you shift from just doing the work to thinking about the work.

**What this phase looks like:**

- **Handle your responsibilities confidently.** The routine should feel like a routine now. Timekeeping, onboarding follow-up, recruiting coordination, filing — these are your lanes and you're comfortable in them.
- **Build relationships intentionally.** Other departments rely on HR. The supervisors you email every week about timecards, the new hires you onboard, the IT contacts you coordinate with — these relationships matter. When you need a fast answer or context on a tricky situation later, the connections you build now are what make that possible.
- **Spot things that could be better.** If an SOP doesn't match reality, say something. If a process has a gap that keeps causing the same issue, flag it. If you've found a faster way to do something without cutting corners, share it. The goal isn't just to do the job — it's to make the job better.
- **Prepare for your 90-day check-in.** This is coming. Start thinking about what's going well, where you want more support, and what you want to grow into. Write it down as you go — you'll forget the details if you wait until the night before.

:::callout tip
Your fresh perspective is an asset. Things that seem odd or inefficient to you in month three might be things everyone else has gotten used to. Speak up — that's valuable.
:::

**How to know you're on track at day 90:** You're a reliable, trusted part of the team. People come to you with questions because they know you'll either have the answer or find it quickly. You're not just executing tasks — you're anticipating what needs to happen next.

## Your 90-Day Check-In

At the end of your first three months, you'll sit down with Nicole for a check-in. This isn't a pass/fail exam — it's a two-way conversation. She'll share feedback on how you're doing, and you'll have the chance to share yours.

**Come prepared to discuss:**

- What's going well and what you feel confident about
- Where you want more support, training, or development
- Questions about your role, expectations, or growth path
- Anything you've noticed about processes or workflows that you think could improve

This conversation is more productive when you've been keeping notes throughout the 90 days. The things you noticed in week two that seemed small? Some of those are worth bringing up. The system you struggled with in week four that now feels easy? That's progress worth naming.

## When You're Not Sure

During your first 90 days, your supervisor is always your first resource. There is no question too small — especially in HR, where acting on a wrong assumption can affect someone's pay, benefits, or employment status.

**The rule is simple:** if you're not sure, stop and ask. A confident wrong answer is worse than a five-minute delay to check.

- **HR policy questions** → Nicole
- **System access or technical issues** → Start with your supervisor, escalate to IT if needed
- **"Should I be handling this?"** → If you're asking the question, the answer is probably no. Escalate it.

Don't wait until something becomes a problem to ask about it. The people who ramp up fastest aren't the ones who figure everything out alone — they're the ones who ask the right questions early and build from there.
