## Building on the Basics

Safety at AAP introduced the universal expectations. This module applies them to the warehouse floor — where the hazards are more specific, the stakes are immediate, and "I'll be careful" is not a safety plan.

Before you step onto the floor, you need to know what protective equipment you are required to wear, how to handle materials safely, what to do in an emergency, and how to report it when something goes wrong.

## Why Safety Comes First

Warehouse work is active, fast-paced, and physical. There is moving equipment, heavy product, tight spaces, and a lot happening at once. That is exactly why safety matters.

At AAP, safety is not the boring part before the real work starts. Safety is part of the real work. Every person in the building deserves a workplace where people pay attention, follow the rules, and look out for each other.

Nobody gets bonus points for rushing into a bad decision. The goal is not just to get the work done. The goal is to get it done safely, correctly, and consistently.

If something feels unsafe, stop and say something. That is not overreacting. That is doing your job.

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

PPE is not optional flair. It is part of being ready to work.

The PPE requirements for the warehouse floor are straightforward. Know them, follow them every day.

**Required for all warehouse employees:**

- **Closed-toe, closed-heel shoes** — required at all times on the warehouse floor. No exceptions.

**Provided as needed:**

- **Box cutters** — issued by API. Handle and store them properly. Do not leave them open or unattended.
- **Gloves** — available by request. If you need gloves for your work, ask your supervisor.

That is the full list of PPE requirements. If a specific task or area ever requires additional protection, your supervisor will tell you before you start.

If your footwear does not meet the requirement, do not come onto the floor until it does.

## Hazard Communication (HazCom / GHS)

If you work around chemicals, cleaning products, or other hazardous materials, you have a right to know what they are, what risks they carry, and how to handle them safely. That is what Hazard Communication is for.

### Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

Every hazardous substance used in the workplace has a Safety Data Sheet — also called an SDS. It explains what the material is, what hazards it presents, what protective steps to take, and what to do if there is a spill or exposure.

**SDS location:** All Safety Data Sheets and hazard communications are available in BambooHR. If you need to look up a product, that is where to go.

### Labels

Hazardous materials must be properly labeled. Never remove, cover, or ignore a label. If a label is missing or damaged, do not use the container — tell your supervisor.

### GHS Pictograms

You may see standard hazard symbols on labels. These pictograms quickly indicate whether a product is flammable, corrosive, toxic, or otherwise hazardous. If you are unfamiliar with these symbols, review the GHS reference materials in BambooHR.

:::callout warning
If you are ever unsure what a chemical is or how to handle it, stop and ask before using it. Guessing and chemicals are a bad combination.
:::

## Safe Lifting and Material Handling

Back injuries are common in warehouse work, and most of them are preventable. Safe lifting is not about being tough. It is about being smart enough to keep doing your job tomorrow.

Before lifting anything, assess the load. If it looks too heavy, too bulky, or too awkward to handle safely alone, get help or use equipment.

**When lifting:**

- Stand close to the load
- Keep your feet about shoulder-width apart
- Bend at the knees, not the waist
- Keep your back straight
- Hold the load close to your body
- Lift with your legs
- Do not twist while lifting — pivot your feet to turn
- Set the load down using the same safe form

**Weight limit for single-person lifts:** 100 lbs. Anything over 100 lbs requires two people — do not attempt it alone.

**Equipment available:** Pallet jacks and forklifts are available in the warehouse. Both require completion of a certification course before you are authorized to operate them. If you are not yet certified, do not use either — not even for a quick move.

:::callout tip
Your back has no interest in being the hero of the shift. Use help when you need it — that is what it is there for.
:::

## Equipment and Forklift Safety

Warehouse equipment makes the job possible. It can also make the day go sideways fast if it is used incorrectly.

AAP uses both forklifts and pallet jacks in the warehouse. Only employees who have completed the certification course are authorized to operate either. No exceptions.

:::callout warning
Operating a forklift or pallet jack without current certification is a serious safety violation. Not even for a second. Not even to "just move it real quick."
:::

**To become certified:** Speak to your supervisor about completing the certification course. Do not operate either piece of equipment until you have completed it and have been authorized.

### Forklift Path Awareness

Even if you never operate a forklift, you need to know where the forklift paths are.

The active forklift routes are marked on the warehouse floor. All employees are expected to:

- Know where the marked paths are in their work area
- Avoid standing or grouping in those areas
- Stay alert when working near active equipment zones

This applies to everyone on the floor — not just operators.

### General Equipment Rules

Before using any equipment:

- Inspect it first
- Do not use damaged or malfunctioning equipment — report the problem to your supervisor immediately

While operating or working near equipment:

- Follow all posted speed limits
- Use designated traffic patterns
- Give pedestrians the right of way
- Sound the horn at intersections or blind corners
- Never carry riders unless the equipment is designed for it

If you are unsure whether something is safe to operate, that is your answer. Stop and ask.

In a life-threatening emergency, call 911 first.

### Fire Emergency

If there is a fire or fire alarm:

1. Stop work immediately
2. Follow the evacuation route
3. Exit the building quickly and calmly
4. Go to **Safe Zone 4**
5. Wait for instructions — do not re-enter until authorized

### Severe Weather / Tornado

If severe weather procedures are activated:

1. Stop work
2. Go to **Safe Zone 4**
3. Stay there until you are told it is safe to leave

### Medical Emergency

If a coworker is having a medical emergency:

- Notify a supervisor immediately
- Call for emergency help if needed
- Do not move the person unless there is immediate danger
- Follow site procedure for first aid and emergency response

**First aid stations and AEDs** are located near the water stations throughout the warehouse and in high-traffic areas of the building. Take a few minutes on your first days to locate the ones nearest your work area.

Emergencies are not the time to improvise. Know the plan before you need it.

## Incident and Injury Reporting

Every injury, incident, and near-miss must be reported immediately. This is not about blame. It is about getting help quickly, fixing hazards, and preventing the next incident.

**Your supervisor is always your first contact.** For any injury, incident, or near-miss, notify your supervisor immediately — before anything else, unless you need emergency medical attention first.

**If you are injured at work:**

1. Stop working
2. Get medical attention if needed
3. Notify your supervisor immediately — do not wait until the end of the shift
4. Your supervisor will guide you through the next steps
5. Contact HR if a workers' compensation claim is needed

Do not try to handle it on your own. Do not assume it is too minor to mention. Your supervisor needs to know, and they are the right person to help you from there.

### Near-Misses Matter Too

A near-miss is an event that could have caused injury or damage, even if it did not. Report these to your supervisor as well.

Near-misses are one of the best early warning systems a workplace has. They show where the next real injury could come from. Reporting them helps fix the problem before someone gets hurt.

## Drug and Alcohol Policy

In a warehouse environment, impairment is a safety risk. No employee may work under the influence of alcohol, illegal substances, or any substance that affects their ability to work safely — including prescription medication if it affects alertness, coordination, judgment, or reaction time.

If you are taking a medication that may affect your ability to work safely, notify your supervisor before your shift.

## Key Takeaways

Safety at AAP is not extra. It is built into the job.

Wear the required PPE. Lift smart. Use equipment correctly. Know the emergency plan. Report injuries and near-misses immediately. Never work impaired. When in doubt, stop and ask.

That is not just compliance. That is how good teams work.
