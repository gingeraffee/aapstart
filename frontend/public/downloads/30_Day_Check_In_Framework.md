# 30-Day Check-In Framework — Warehouse Employees

## Purpose

The 30-day check-in is a structured, developmental conversation between a supervisor and a new warehouse employee. It is not a disciplinary tool and it is not a performance review — it is a checkpoint designed to surface what is working, what is not, and what needs to happen next.

The goal is simple: make sure the employee has the foundation they need to succeed, and give them clear direction for the next 60 days.

---

## Before the Check-In

Prepare by reviewing the following before you sit down with the employee:

- **Attendance and punctuality** — Pull their attendance record. Note any patterns (late arrivals, unplanned absences). This is not about punishment — it is about catching trends early.
- **Trainer feedback** — Talk to the trainer(s) who worked with them during their first week. What stood out? Where did they struggle? How did they respond to coaching?
- **Safety observations** — Have you or others noticed consistent PPE compliance? Any safety concerns or close calls?
- **General observations** — How do they interact with the team? Are they asking questions? Do they seem engaged or checked out?

---

## During the Check-In

Work through each section below. You do not need to follow this word for word, but every section should be covered.

### 1. Set the Tone

Start by telling the employee what this conversation is and what it is not. They may be nervous — especially if no one has told them what to expect.

Say something like: "This is a 30-day check-in. It is not a test and you are not in trouble. I want to hear how things are going from your side, walk through a few things together, and set some goals for the next couple of months."

### 2. Hands-On Knowledge Check

Walk through the core tasks the employee has been trained on. This should be practical and conversational — not a written test.

**What to assess:**
- Can they walk you through their primary daily tasks step by step?
- Do they understand the scanning and inventory processes they have been trained on?
- Can they identify when something is wrong and explain what they would do about it?
- Are they following proper procedures or taking shortcuts?

**What to look for:**
- Confidence vs. hesitation — do they know the process or are they guessing?
- Safety awareness woven into their explanation (PPE, proper lifting, hazard recognition)
- Willingness to say "I am not sure about that part" instead of faking it

**If gaps are found:** Note them. These become part of the training plan for days 31 through 90. Do not treat gaps as failures — treat them as information.

### 3. Employee Self-Assessment

Ask the employee to talk through how they feel about the job so far. Use open-ended questions:

- "What parts of the job feel the most natural to you right now?"
- "Where do you still feel like you are figuring things out?"
- "Is there anything you were trained on that you have not actually had to do yet?"
- "What has been harder than you expected?"

**Why this matters:** Employees who can honestly assess where they are tend to improve faster. If they say everything is great and you know it is not, that is useful information too — it tells you they may need more direct feedback.

### 4. Safety and Compliance Review

Walk through safety expectations and check for understanding — not just compliance, but comprehension.

**Key areas:**
- **PPE** — Do they wear the correct PPE consistently and without reminders?
- **Emergency procedures** — Can they describe what to do in a fire, chemical spill, or injury on the floor?
- **Hazard recognition** — Can they identify common hazards in their work area?
- **Incident reporting** — Do they know how and when to report a safety concern?

**If there are concerns:** Address them directly and document them. Safety is non-negotiable, and the employee needs to understand that clearly.

### 5. Resource and Systems Awareness

Check whether the employee can navigate the tools and systems they need:

- **BambooHR** — Can they submit a PTO request? Do they know where to find their pay stubs?
- **Contacts** — Do they know who their HR contact is? Their IT contact? Their trainer?
- **Policies** — Do they know where to find the employee handbook and other policy documents?

If they cannot do these things, it is a training gap — not a character flaw. Flag it and make sure they get the help they need.

### 6. Team and Culture Fit

This is less about "do they fit in" and more about "do they feel supported."

- Are they comfortable asking questions of coworkers and trainers?
- Have they had any interpersonal issues or concerns?
- Do they feel like part of the team, or are they still on the outside looking in?

**Listen carefully here.** New employees often will not bring up concerns unprompted. Ask directly and give them space to answer honestly.

### 7. Coachability and Growth Observations

Share your own observations about how the employee responds to feedback and direction:

- When corrected, do they adjust or resist?
- Are they applying what they learn, or do you find yourself repeating the same things?
- Do they take initiative, or do they wait to be told what to do next?

**Be specific.** Instead of "you need to be more proactive," try "I noticed when you finished your pick list on Tuesday, you waited 10 minutes before asking what to do next. In that situation, check in with me or start on the next task."

### 8. Attendance and Punctuality

Review the attendance record you pulled before the meeting:

- Acknowledge good attendance if it has been consistent
- If there are patterns (late starts, early departures, frequent call-outs), address them honestly
- Ask if there are any barriers to consistent attendance that you or HR can help with

**Keep it constructive.** The goal is awareness and course correction, not discipline — unless the pattern is already at a policy threshold.

### 9. Training Gap Identification

Based on everything you have discussed, identify:

- What processes or tasks need a refresher?
- Is there anything the employee has not been trained on yet that they should be?
- Are there areas where extended trainer support would help?

Document these gaps clearly — they become the foundation of the days 31 through 90 plan.

### 10. Goal-Setting for Days 31 Through 90

Set two to four specific, measurable goals for the next 60 days. These should come from the conversation — not from a template.

**Good goals look like:**
- "By day 60, complete all pick tasks independently without trainer support"
- "Reduce scanning errors to fewer than two per week by day 75"
- "Complete forklift certification by day 45"
- "Demonstrate proper spill response procedure during next safety drill"

**Avoid vague goals like:**
- "Get better at the job"
- "Be more careful"
- "Improve attitude"

Write the goals down and give the employee a copy. These are what you will revisit at the 90-day check-in.

### 11. Employee Feedback on Onboarding

Ask the employee for honest feedback on their onboarding and training experience:

- "What part of your first month helped you the most?"
- "Was there anything that felt like a waste of time?"
- "Is there something that would have helped you learn faster?"
- "Did you feel like you had the support you needed?"

**Take notes.** This feedback is valuable for improving the onboarding process for future hires. Pass it along to HR and the training team.

---

## After the Check-In

### Document the Conversation

Record the following:
- Key strengths observed
- Gaps or concerns identified
- Goals set for days 31 through 90
- Any follow-up actions (additional training, equipment needs, HR referrals)
- Employee feedback on onboarding

### Follow Up

- Share a summary with the employee so they have a record of their goals
- Notify HR of any concerns that need support (attendance issues, training gaps, interpersonal concerns)
- Schedule a brief informal check-in at the 60-day mark to assess progress on goals
- Plan for the formal 90-day check-in

- [ ] Completed hands-on knowledge check

- [ ] Reviewed safety and compliance
- [ ] Checked resource and systems awareness
- [ ] Discussed team and culture fit
- [ ] Shared coachability and growth observations
- [ ] Addressed attendance and punctuality
- [ ] Identified training gaps
- [ ] Set 2 to 4 specific goals for days 31 through 90
- [ ] Collected employee feedback on onboarding
- [ ] Documented conversation and outcomes
- [ ] Shared summary with employee
- [ ] Notified HR of any follow-up items
